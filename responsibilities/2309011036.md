## 1. Variational Autoencoder

### From ELBO to a trainable loss

The marginal likelihood `p(x)` is intractable, so we maximise its variational lower bound (ELBO):

$$\log p(x) \;\geq\; \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x)\,\|\,p(z))$$

Equivalently we *minimise* the negative ELBO:

$$\mathcal{L}_\text{VAE}(x) = \underbrace{-\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)]}_{\text{reconstruction}} + \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(0,I))}_{\text{regulariser}}$$

I use **per-pixel BCE** as the reconstruction term (matching Sigmoid output ∈ [0,1]) and the **closed-form Gaussian KL** for diagonal `q ~ N(μ, σ²)` against standard normal `p ~ N(0, I)`:

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{d}\big(1 + \log\sigma_i^2 - \mu_i^2 - \sigma_i^2\big)$$

Both terms are summed over pixels/dims and **averaged over batch** — so the loss has the same scale across batch sizes.

### Reparameterisation trick

Direct sampling `z ~ N(μ, σ²)` is non-differentiable in `μ, σ`. Instead I write:

$$z = \mu + \sigma \odot \epsilon, \quad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$

The randomness now lives in `ε` (which we don't need a gradient through), and `μ, σ` are differentiable. Implemented as `VAE.reparameterize(mu, logvar)`. We parameterise `log σ²` instead of `σ` so the network output is unconstrained and `σ = exp(0.5 · log σ²) > 0` automatically.

### Architecture

I reused Person 3's `ConvAE` shape and replaced the bottleneck linear with two parallel heads `fc_mu` and `fc_logvar`. Latent dim = 20 (slightly smaller than the deterministic AE — VAEs are easier to train into a clean prior at modest dims). The decoder is identical to Person 3's upsample-then-conv stack.

### What the VAE buys

1. **Sample-from-the-prior generation** — `z ~ N(0, I)` ➜ realistic-looking Fashion-MNIST images. The vanilla AE cannot do this because its latent space has no meaningful density. See `10_vae_samples.png`.
2. **Smooth latent interpolation** — linearly interpolating between two posterior means yields a continuous morph between the two source images. See `11_vae_interp.png`.
3. **Tighter t-SNE clusters** — the KL regulariser pulls the per-sample posteriors toward each other, giving cleaner unsupervised class separation than the vanilla AE. See `12_vae_tsne.png` (compare to Person 3's `09_ae_tsne.png`).

## 2. Deep Convolutional GAN (DCGAN)

### Minimax objective

A GAN is a two-player game. The discriminator `D` learns to tell real from fake; the generator `G` learns to fool `D`:

$$\min_G \max_D \;\mathbb{E}_{x\sim p_\text{data}}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z\sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$$

In practice we replace the second term with the **non-saturating loss** for `G`: `−E[log D(G(z))]`. This avoids vanishing gradients when `D` is much stronger than `G` early in training.

### DCGAN rules (Radford, Metz & Chintala, 2015)

I followed the four canonical rules to the letter:

1. **No max-pool.** Strided convolutions in `D`, transposed convolutions in `G`.
2. **BatchNorm in both networks** — except the first layer of `D` and the last layer of `G`, which Radford et al. show would otherwise destabilise training.
3. **LeakyReLU(0.2) in `D`**, **ReLU in `G`**, **Tanh on G's output** to match images normalised to `[-1, 1]`.
4. **Adam β₁ = 0.5** instead of the default 0.9 — vanilla momentum oscillates the adversarial dynamics.

### Stability tricks I applied

- **One-sided label smoothing** — real-label = 0.9 instead of 1.0. Prevents `D` from becoming over-confident, which would shrink `G`'s gradient. *Only* the real label is smoothed (Salimans et al., 2016 explicitly argue against smoothing fakes).
- **`BCEWithLogitsLoss`** instead of separate Sigmoid + BCE — numerically stable in float32.
- **Custom DCGAN init** — `Conv*` weights `~ N(0, 0.02)`, `BatchNorm` weights `~ N(1, 0.02)`. This is the exact init from the DCGAN paper.
- **Fixed-z probe** — the same `(64, 100)` noise tensor is decoded after every epoch, so `14_dcgan_progression.png` shows the *same* 64 generated images sharpening across epochs. This is the standard qualitative GAN-progress visualisation.

### What I deliberately left out (future work)

- **Spectral normalisation** would tighten Lipschitz constants on `D` and remove the need for BatchNorm. I left it out to keep the focus on textbook DCGAN behaviour.
- **WGAN / WGAN-GP** with the Wasserstein loss would give a more interpretable training curve. Same reasoning.
- **Two-time-scale update rule** (different learning rates for G and D) — useful on harder datasets, unnecessary on Fashion-MNIST.

## 3. VAE vs DCGAN — the headline comparison

This is the table in REPORT.md §5.5. The short version:

| Property | VAE | DCGAN |
|---|---|---|
| Training curve | Smooth, monotonic | Oscillating, no single loss to read |
| Sample sharpness | Slightly blurry (averaging effect of MSE/BCE under Gaussian decoder) | Sharper |
| Likelihood | Tractable lower bound | None |
| Mode coverage | Good | Mode-collapse risk |
| Best when you want | Controllable latent space, anomaly detection | Visually convincing samples |

Both models are *correct* — they answer different questions about the same data. Modern systems hybridise the two strengths (NVAE, VQ-VAE+autoregressive prior, latent diffusion).
